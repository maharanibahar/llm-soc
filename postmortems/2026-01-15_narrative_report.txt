Security Incident Report

Executive Summary

On January 15, 2026, our web application experienced a series of coordinated cyberattacks over a brief 17-second window. An attacker attempted to exploit vulnerabilities in our search functionality, feedback system, and error handling pages using a technique called cross-site scripting, or XSS. While our security systems detected all four attack attempts and successfully blocked two of them, two attacks reached our application before being stopped, raising concerns about potential data exposure.

The incident began at 10:00 AM UTC when suspicious activity was detected across multiple endpoints. Our automated monitoring systems immediately flagged the attacks, and our security team began investigating within minutes. Fortunately, all services remained operational throughout the incident, and we have no evidence at this time that customer data was compromised or that any users were harmed. However, one attack may have successfully stored malicious code in our feedback system, which we are actively investigating and addressing.

We take this incident seriously and are committed to transparency. This report explains what happened, what we learned, and the concrete steps we are taking to strengthen our defenses. We believe that sharing these lessons openly helps not only our own organization but also contributes to the broader security community's understanding of emerging threats.

Background

Cross-site scripting is a type of cyberattack where malicious actors attempt to inject harmful scripts into websites that other users visit. Think of it like someone slipping a note into a public bulletin board that, when read by the next person, causes their browser to perform unwanted actions. These scripts can potentially steal login credentials, hijack user sessions, or redirect visitors to fraudulent websites. XSS attacks are among the most common web vulnerabilities and have been a known threat for over two decades.

In this incident, the attacker targeted four different parts of our web application: the search feature, a feedback submission form, a URL redirection service, and our error message pages. Each of these endpoints accepts user input and displays it back, which is a normal function for web applications. However, if that input is not properly validated and sanitized, it can become a vector for XSS attacks.

Our security infrastructure includes multiple layers of defense. We operate an intrusion detection system called Wazuh, which monitors network traffic in real-time and alerts us to suspicious activity. We also deploy a Web Application Firewall, or WAF, which acts as a gatekeeper, inspecting incoming requests and blocking those that match known attack patterns. Additionally, our applications are designed to validate and sanitize user input before processing it. As this incident revealed, however, there were gaps in how these layers worked together.

What Happened

The incident unfolded rapidly on the morning of January 15, 2026. At exactly 10:00:00 AM UTC, our intrusion detection system flagged the first suspicious request. The attacker sent a GET request to our search endpoint, attempting to inject a script tag into the search query parameter. Our Wazuh monitoring system immediately detected this as a cross-site scripting attack and generated an alert. However, our Web Application Firewall did not respond to this request, allowing it to reach our application. The application processed the request and returned a standard response, which means the malicious script may have been executed in the browser of anyone viewing that search results page.

Six seconds later, at 10:00:06 AM, the attacker escalated their campaign by targeting our feedback API. This time, they attempted a more dangerous variant of the attack: stored cross-site scripting. Instead of just reflecting malicious code back to the immediate user, this attack aimed to write the malicious script into our database, where it could potentially affect any future user who views the feedback content. Our intrusion detection system once again identified the attack, this time with a higher severity rating. Our Web Application Firewall detected the malicious payload with high confidence and blocked the request. However, our application had already processed the request and returned a success response before the firewall could intervene. This timing issue means the malicious content may have been written to our database, and we are conducting a thorough investigation to determine the extent of any potential persistence.

At 10:00:09 AM, just three seconds after the previous attack, the attacker shifted tactics again. They targeted our URL redirection service, attempting to exploit a different variant of cross-site scripting that operates entirely in the user's browser, known as DOM-based XSS. This technique manipulates the way web pages dynamically update their content without sending the malicious payload to the server. Our intrusion detection system caught this attempt, and our Web Application Firewall successfully blocked it before it could reach our application. This was the first attack in the sequence that was completely prevented from reaching our systems.

The final attack occurred at 10:00:17 AM, eight seconds after the previous attempt. The attacker targeted our error handling pages, attempting to inject malicious code through the error message parameter. Our intrusion detection system identified this as another cross-site scripting attempt, though with a lower severity rating than the previous attacks. Our Web Application Firewall detected the malicious payload but, due to its confidence scoring system, only logged the event without blocking it. The application processed the request and returned an error response, but again, the malicious script may have been executed before the error was displayed.

Looking at the pattern across all four attacks, it is clear that the attacker was methodically testing different endpoints and attack vectors. They started with a simple reflected attack to probe our defenses, then escalated to a more dangerous stored attack, tried a browser-based variant, and finally tested our error handling. The rapid succession of attacks, all within 17 seconds, suggests the use of automated scanning tools designed to systematically identify vulnerabilities. Our intrusion detection system tracked this escalating pattern, with its alert counter increasing from 1 to 10 across the four events, indicating that each attack was building on the previous one.

What We Learned

Our incident response revealed both strengths and weaknesses in our security posture. On the positive side, our intrusion detection system performed exceptionally well, identifying all four attack attempts with appropriate severity ratings. This demonstrates that our signature-based detection rules are comprehensive and up-to-date. The system provided real-time alerting that enabled our security team to respond quickly and begin investigating within minutes of the first attack. This rapid detection is critical for minimizing the window of opportunity for attackers.

Our Web Application Firewall successfully blocked two of the four attacks, preventing those malicious payloads from reaching our application. The blocked attacks included the stored XSS attempt and the DOM-based XSS variant, both of which were identified with high confidence scores. This shows that when properly configured, our WAF is effective at stopping known attack patterns.

However, the incident also exposed significant gaps in our defenses. The most concerning issue is that our Web Application Firewall did not respond to the first attack at all. This suggests that our WAF rules for the search endpoint do not adequately inspect URL query parameters for cross-site scripting payloads. The firewall may be focused on POST request bodies and known attack signatures in specific request components, but it lacks comprehensive coverage for all parts of HTTP requests, particularly URL query strings.

Another critical issue is the inconsistency in how our Web Application Firewall enforces its blocking policies. The firewall uses a confidence scoring system to determine how likely a request is to be malicious. When the score is high, it blocks the request. When the score is moderate, it only logs the event without blocking. In this incident, the firewall blocked attacks with scores of 95 and 89, but only detected an attack with a score of 60. This inconsistency allowed the final attack to proceed unchallenged, despite being clearly malicious. Attackers can exploit this by crafting simpler payloads that score below the blocking threshold while still being effective.

The most serious finding is that our applications do not adequately validate or sanitize user input before processing it. All four attacks reached our application logic and were processed without any security checks. Our architecture relies too heavily on perimeter defenses like the Web Application Firewall, with insufficient validation at the application layer itself. This violates a fundamental security principle called defense-in-depth, which requires multiple independent layers of security controls. When one layer fails, as our WAF did on the first and fourth attacks, there should be additional layers to catch the threat.

Additionally, the timing issue with the stored XSS attack reveals an architectural flaw. Our application processed the request and wrote data to the database before the Web Application Firewall could terminate the connection. This race condition means that even when the WAF blocks a request, malicious data may have already been persisted. This is particularly dangerous for stored cross-site scripting, which can affect all users who view the compromised content, not just the initial attacker.

Our Response

We are taking immediate and comprehensive action to address the vulnerabilities identified in this incident. First and foremost, we are conducting a thorough investigation of our feedback database to determine whether the stored XSS payload was successfully persisted. If malicious content is found, we will remove it immediately and notify any potentially affected users. This investigation is our highest priority and is being completed within 24 hours of the incident.

We are also auditing all four affected endpoints to determine whether any of the reflected XSS attacks successfully executed malicious scripts. We are reviewing application logs to identify whether any users accessed compromised content during the incident window. This analysis will help us understand the full scope of potential impact and take appropriate remediation steps.

To prevent similar incidents in the future, we are implementing several technical improvements. We are updating our Web Application Firewall rules to ensure comprehensive coverage of all HTTP request components, including URL query parameters, for every endpoint. We are also lowering the blocking threshold so that attacks with moderate confidence scores are blocked rather than merely detected. This will eliminate the inconsistency that allowed the final attack to proceed.

Perhaps most importantly, we are implementing robust input validation and output encoding at the application layer. Every endpoint that accepts user input will be updated to validate that input against a strict whitelist of allowed characters and patterns before processing it. We are also implementing output encoding to ensure that any user-supplied content is safely rendered in web pages, preventing script execution even if malicious content somehow bypasses our other defenses.

We are repositioning our Web Application Firewall to ensure it inspects and blocks requests before they reach our application logic, eliminating the race condition that allowed the stored XSS attack to potentially persist data. This architectural change will ensure that malicious requests are stopped before they can be processed by our applications.

To detect and deter automated scanning tools like the one used in this incident, we are implementing rate limiting that will restrict the number of requests from a single IP address within a given time window. We are also adding automatic IP blocking after multiple failed attack attempts, which will significantly increase the cost and difficulty for attackers attempting to systematically probe our defenses.

Looking Forward

This incident has been a valuable learning experience for our organization. While we are disappointed that gaps in our defenses allowed two attacks to succeed, we are grateful that our monitoring systems detected the incident quickly and that we have been able to identify and address the root causes. The rapid response of our security team, combined with the comprehensive investigation we have conducted, has given us a clear understanding of what went wrong and how to fix it.

We have learned that perimeter defenses alone are not sufficient to protect against sophisticated attacks. The principle of defense-in-depth, which requires multiple independent layers of security controls, is not just a theoretical best practice but a practical necessity. We are committed to implementing this principle across all of our systems, ensuring that no single point of failure can compromise our security posture.

We have also learned the importance of consistent security policies. Inconsistent enforcement, whether in Web Application Firewall blocking thresholds or application-layer validation, creates exploitable gaps that determined attackers will find and exploit. We are standardizing our security policies to ensure that obvious attacks are always blocked, regardless of their complexity or the confidence score assigned by our detection systems.

Most importantly, we have reaffirmed our commitment to transparency. We believe that sharing information about security incidents, including our mistakes and the lessons we have learned, helps strengthen the entire security community. We are publishing this report openly and will provide updates as we complete our remediation efforts. We welcome feedback from the security community and encourage other organizations to share their own experiences so that we can all learn from each other.

We are committed to continuous improvement of our security posture. The action items identified in this report are being tracked and will be completed according to their priority levels. We will conduct a follow-up review in seven days to assess our progress and identify any additional improvements needed. We are also scheduling a comprehensive penetration test to identify any additional vulnerabilities that may not have been revealed by this incident.

Thank you for your trust and understanding. We take our responsibility to protect your data seriously, and we are committed to earning and maintaining that trust through transparent communication and continuous improvement.
